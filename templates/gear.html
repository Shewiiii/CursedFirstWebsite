Sound quality is a myth.

(February 8th, 2023)

We often think that expensive products are better, that expensive headphones, speakers or IEMs have a better sound quality, or are more neutral. But let's step back for a second and ask ourselves: what really is "sound quality"? Is it even objectively definable? Is "neutral sound" even a thing? Is there even a correlation between price and "sound quality"?
Hint: the answer is in the title..

Let's be honest: the audio world is full of marketing bullshit, and makes you believe that you need things in order to have a better audio experience (like Hi-res files [which I briefly talk about on this page], or IEMs with lots of drivers). But how can you tell the true from the false? Why is it that when some people describe certain headphones as having incredible sound quality, others find it unlistenable? Okay, that's a lot of questions, and to answer them, we need to go back to the basics. If you'd like to get your head around all this fog, then read on. I promise you: it's going to be interesting!

2. Describe sound objectively

How can you even measure the sound of headphones ?

Because yeah, before talking about sound quality, we have to know what it's based on!

Harmonic distortion

Let's talk about the least important first: harmonic distortion. Basically, as soon as we find harmonics that either shouldn't be there in a sound, or have the wrong sound level, it is harmonic distortion. Since headphones or speakers are physical systems, this is somewhat unavoidable, as the driver in them will not be able to move exactly according to the electric signal received.

In practique though, harmonic distortion in headphones is 99% of the time inaudible, because its level is very low and pretty much random. However, If you input a signal with an excessive level (of bass for exemple, that can be done with EQ), harmonic distortion starts to matter more, since increasing sound level will this will inevitably make the distortion more audible.
If the driver isn't capable of producing a certain frequency range at a satisfactory sound level, you'll need to EQ a lot. So much that harmonic distinction will be an issue. An example of this is open-back headphones, where, because of the sound leaking, the bass tends to roll off significantly (cf. Doc.2). If you try to compensate it with EQ, the sound will probably be weird.

Frequency response

Now comes the most important element when describing sound: frequency response! As its name implies, it corresponds to the sound level over a given frequency range (usually from 20Hz to 20kHz since they correspond to the limits of human hearing). Easy right ? Well..not really. The fact that there are hundreds of ways to represent frequency response of a headphone is already a red flag.

If you want, you can compare graphs from Crinacle's database, Squiglink, Rtings or Soundguys: even if its the same headphones, the graphs will be different. I will explain why in the next section.

In any case, if you had to remember only one thing, it would be this: never read a FR graph without knowledge of the circumstances of the measurement.

Doc.2: A representation (among many others) of FR (frequency response) of the Sennheiser HD650. Compensated graph. Rig: B&K 5128. Target: Diffuse Field. Tilt: -1dB/octave. Source

What about all the other metrics ? Some websites show many more graphs!

This section is a bit more advanced and theoretical.

In one sentence, all the other metrics, like waterfall/CSD plots (descibing the decay of a sound at a given frequency range, cf. Doc.3,4), squarewave responses or group delay, are both completely useless, as they are not at all representative of subjective experience, and irrelevant, as they are intrinsically linked to frequency response. Lemme explain.

Headphones are linear systems.

Basically, One certain input -> One certain output. A headphone will always behave the same way since its a passive system. The membrane that makes sound in a headphone moves according to the electric current it receives.

This also means that headphones are minimum phase. To explain what it is and what does it involve, let's say you do an impulse response test with a headphone (i.e. the measurement of membrane movement while inputing a very sudden variation in the signal, on a extremely short period of time).

  • You will not be able to see any pre-echo, since the membrane cant move before the signal reachs the headphone
  • But you will see a decay of the impulse/a post-echo.
  • All frequencies aren't produced at the same time/with the same delay, and can move in time. That what we call group delay. This isn't de case with linear phase systems, and it affects the timbre of the signal. It isn't related with subjective perception of imaging as rtings tries to make you believe..

One important property of minimum phase systems is that they behaves consistently between time and frequency domains. This is very important, because it means that 2 systems (like 2 headphones) behaving the exact same way in the frequency domain will also behave the same way in the time domain if they have the same acoustic output impedance (see next section). I could stop here, as this property explains just about everything !

Let's give some examples nonetheless:

  • Frequency response has the biggest impact on sound and distortions in headphones are negligible as long as they are not awful
  • CSD plots: EQing the headphone modifies and can remove decay of the CSD plot like below

    • Doc.3: CSD plot of the HD800 before EQ.

    Doc.4: CSD plot of the HD800 after EQ.

  • Squarewave: EQing (with minimum phase EQ) a headphone to have the same FR as another headphone results in the same squarewave response
  • Impulse response: EQing (with minimum phase EQ) a headphone to have the same FR as another headphone results in the same Impulse response

Funny pattern right ?

All this to say that even if the sound can be described with these metrics, there is really no point in using them because they give the same information as the frequency response, but in a way that makes it almost impossible to interpret subjective experience (what we are really hearing).

Tl;dr: Frequency response is the only metric you should care about, unless you do some hardcore EQ. In that case, harmonic distortion graphs can be useful.

3. Variation nightmare

So what's the catch then ?

Have you ever wondered how it's possible for there to be so many contradictory opinions on the same headphone ? If you take a look at some internet forums, you'll understand what I'm talking about, but when we're talking about a good monitor for example, this variation is much less important. So what's the problem ?

Preferences doesn't explain the full story: we all hear differently. Like literally.

You see where I'm going with this. That's why the idea of "neutral sound" is a bit stupid at the first place, but more on that later. But then, what is it that really varies from one person to the next ?

Our hearing isn't linear in Sound level vs. Frequency because of human anatomy

Let's do a little experiment. If you're not in a totally silent environment, put your hands behind your ears, so that they look like elephant ears, and listen carefully. Do it.

Do you notice any difference ? Yes, you can hear better upper midrange sounds! All this to say that the auditory pinna among others, modify the sound before it reaches your eardrums. In our case, the pinna picks up some sounds better than other sounds, and in particular sounds between 2khz and 8khz. They are amplified, and more audible to the human ear: that's what will call the ear-gain!

Doc.5: OE (over-ear) Harman Target 2018 on a sound level vs. frequency graph. We can see the ear-gain region by the elevation between around 2kHz and 8kHz. Source

What is an HRTF ?

Basically, an HRTF is a sum of all the alterations that sound receives, whether through our pinna or ear-canal, or even our own torso, before reaching our eardrum. Note that the HRTF varies depending on the sound location. In our case, we are focusing on headphones, so that location variation isn't a problem, but we have to take it into consideration nonetheless. Speakers aren't behaving the same way as headphones, so we can't use the same target for example.

Please remember that nobody has the same HRTF, this is physically impossible. All heads and bodies are different, and that variation even exists between your two ears!

What is acoustic impedance ?

An oversimplified definition of acoustic impedance would be that it refers to the resistance exerted by a medium (the structure of the headphones, your ear-canal...) on a sound signal. Acoustic impedance in headphones varies with frequency. All headphones have a different acoustic output impedance, while all humans have a different acoustic input impedance.

In a headphone, the higher the acoustic output impedance, the higher the sound variation will be. For example, IEMs have a very high output impedence (because it's basically a closed box). Thus, different positioning or a different HRTF will result in a drastically different sound.

For now, just remember that acoustic impedance affects the sound until reaches our auditory eardrum. Because it is frequency-dependent, it removes any form of consistency. For example, if 2 headphones have the same frequency response on a measurement rig, since you don't have the same acoustic input impedance as the dummy, and since the two headphones don't have the same acoustic output impedance, the sound will be totally different on your head. This is extremely important, as it is the source of many misconceptions. For example, it is for this reason that EQing a headphone so that it has exactly the same frequency response as another (based on measurements) doesn't result in an identical sound, but rather a very different one to our eardrum, even though headphones are linear systems.

Why does it matter ?

Does this variation really matter that much ? Well, if you're an average person who just wants to listen to music, then not really. On the other hand, when it comes to measuring headphones, HRTF and acoustic impedance are variables that are impossible to ignore. I would go so far as to say they are the reason why headphone measurements are impossible to standardize.

Headphone measurements are impossible to standardize.

Headphones measurements are a mess. Here's why:

The target nightmare.

What is a target ?

Think of a target as an ideal frequency response, more or less arbitrarily defined by someone. They are often represented by a dotted line on graphs. Personally, I distinguish between two types of target:

  • Science-based targets (this name is totally arbitrary). They are based either on scientific research, such as the Harman target, or on measurements in a controlled environment, such as the Diffuse Field (+tilt) target.
  • Preference targets. They are determined arbitrarily, without any real scientific research behind them, but can nevertheless be inspired by them. Rtings and Sonarworks are using this type of target, but many reviewers also use their own targets. In my opinion, preference targets are worthless, due to their cruel lack of precision: since they are arbitrarily defined, they are by definition subjective.

Raw vs. Compensated measurement

  • A raw measurement is a graphical representation of the measured frequency response of a headphone, which has not been compensated for by a target (cf. Doc.5,6,7).
    In other words, what is measured by the measurement rig is displayed directly on the graph. The ear-gain is still visible on a raw measurement (remember that a measurement rig is supposed to simulate human hearing).
  • A compensated measurement is quite the opposite. On a compensated measurement, the target is basically flat, so the graph is much easier to interpret. (cf. Doc.2,9)

For a long time, I thought that raw measurements were better than compensated ones, because the target used was clearly visible on the graph, but I was very much mistaken. Uncompensated measurements should not be used for the following reasons:

  • First, because of the sine illusion, the presence of curves makes the measurement almost impossible to interpret correctly. On the left, you can see 2 curved lines and on the right, the difference in height between the two. When the target is not straight, you can see that it's not really possible to interpret the graph correctly.

    • Doc.11: Curve-difference chart vs. actual curve difference.

  • Then, two raw measurements are not comparable with each other if they don't come from the same rig. This is simply because the measurement is raw, and the HRTF of the rig is therefore not compensated by the target.

Everyone uses a different target.

Basically everyone uses a different target. Because of that, the audio measurements we can find on the internet no longer make any sense, unless you're familiar with the target used.

However, let's talk a little more about the ones that are more relevant to use.

The Harman Target: it is not what you think it is.

Let's be clear: the harman target does not refer to a "neutral" sound and never has. The aim of the research behind the harman target was as follows: to find a target (a headphone frequency response in the case of the over-ear harman target) that appeals to the population AVERAGE. That's about it.
Besides, a model has been set up to predict the percentage chance of an individual liking a certain frequency response. You can check the paper here.

In summary, if a pair of headphones has a frequency response very close to the harman target on compatible systems, this means that there's a good chance that the majority of the population will like the sound of the headphones, but this in NO WAY means that this will be the case for you: this is only a statistic on a population and cannot be applied on an individual scale. Not really related to audio, but it's kind of the same idea as BMI. It has no value at the level of the individual in terms of his or her state of health, but as it's practical to use, it's still widely used.

In addition, you'll notice that the harman target is extremely smoothed so as not to take too much risk in preference prediction. The downside of this smoothing is that it masks more fine-grained information on the target.

Finally, you should be careful if you decide to use the in-ear (IE) harman target, as the conditions of the research leave doubts about the result obtained and the model developed.

The Diffuse Field target: the current standard (in my opinion).

To explain what the Diffuse Field target is, and why this target is trully beautiful, let me put you in context.

Imagine you're in a room with many loudspeakers with a completely neutral frequency response (in an anechoic chamber). This room has the following property: no matter where you are in the room and no matter how you turn your head, the sound will always be the same. This room is called a diffuse field.
A curve, the Diffuse Field curve, can therefore be determined by placing a measurement dummy anywhere in the room, then measuring the room's frequency response by emitting sound through the loudspeakers. This frequency response is no longer neutral, but changed by the properties of the room.

Doc.7: Example of a diffuse field in a laboratory in Germany. Yes it is a bit creepy. Paper source

Now let's think about what headphones are. Headphones consist of two loudspeakers that you place close to your ears, and have the following property: no matter where you are in a room, and no matter how you turn your head, the sound will always be the same.

Interesting right!

It's easy to see why this curve is a good one to use. However, there is a catch. A study from Harman showed that people preferred "a new target that simulates the in-room response of a high-quality loudspeaker system calibrated in a room" over the Diffuse Field curve (Paper source). This is quite logical, since listeners don't listen to music in a diffuse field. But what should we do then ?

The solution is that: apply a "tilt" to the frequency response ! This tilt often used is between -0.8 and -1dB/octave. We attenuate the sound level progressively according to frequency, in a similar way as loudspeakers with totally neutral FR in an anechoic chamber when placed in a normal room. And tada~, the Diffuse Field taget is born!

Everyone uses a different measurement rig.

The main reason why it's impossible to standardize headphone measurements is this: everyone uses different equipment to make measurements, in addition to doing them in their own way. As mentioned above, all heads have a different HRTF and acoustic impedance, and this also applies to dummies. However, some rigs are more accurate than others. The most accurate one in existence is the B&K 5128, because its acoustic input impedence is the closest to a real human.
